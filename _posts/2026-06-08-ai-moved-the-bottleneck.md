Every few months someone publishes the Agile obituary. The cause of death
changes — SAFe killed it, remote work killed it, now AI killed it. I've
stopped holding my breath.

What's different this time is that the "AI killed Agile" crowd has a
real observation buried in a bad conclusion. AI *has* changed something
fundamental about software delivery. It just didn't kill Agile. It moved the
bottleneck — and in doing so, made the parts Agile always cared about more
important, not less.

## Execution used to be the constraint

For most of software's history, the constraint was building the thing. Coding
was slow and expensive. You needed a lot of hands at keyboards for every one
person deciding what those hands should do. That's why engineer-to-PM ratios
settled somewhere between 4:1 and 10:1. The organization shaped itself around
the cost of execution.

AI has started to collapse that ratio. Andrew Ng has noted that the industry
is moving toward something closer to 1:1. A single developer, well-equipped
with the right tools, can produce in an afternoon what used to take a team a
sprint. The execution constraint is weakening fast.

When that happens, delays don't disappear. They surface wherever they were
hiding while the coding was slow enough to mask them.

## The bottleneck was never where you thought it was

Here's the uncomfortable truth: if teams are building faster but lead time
isn't improving proportionally, then engineering output was never the real
bottleneck. It was carrying the weight of everything else — prioritization
ambiguity, approval chains, inability to validate assumptions quickly,
decision latency at every level.

The work that gets stuck today isn't the implementation. It's the questions
before it:

- *Is this the right thing to build?*
- *Who signed off on that?*
- *What does "done" actually mean here?*
- *Did we talk to any customers before building this?*

These aren't new problems. They were just easier to ignore when writing the
code was the long pole. Speed up the code and these questions stop hiding
behind the sprint board. They sit in the open, blocking the next thing.

The new bottleneck is decisions.

## This is precisely where Scrum lives

Scrum never promised to make developers type faster. It was designed around
the problems that remain once you have the bottleneck right:

- Short feedback loops to catch wrong assumptions early
- Explicit moments to inspect and adapt
- A Product Owner who is empowered to make real decisions
- Shared understanding built through refinement and review
- Ceremonies that force the question *"should we still be building this?"*

None of that becomes less relevant when AI accelerates execution. It becomes
more relevant. The competitive advantage no longer lives in how fast you can
ship a feature someone asked for. It lives in how quickly you can figure out
what's actually worth shipping, validate it, and change course when you're
wrong.

Listening to customers, reading the room, challenging assumptions, killing
features that aren't working — these are the activities that Scrum events
were designed to protect. An AI can write the code. It cannot run a
retrospective that changes how a team thinks.

## The org readiness problem

There's a harder version of this problem that most teams run into before they
solve the bottleneck question. If you point AI at a dysfunctional delivery
system, it doesn't fix the dysfunction. It moves faster through it.

Teams with AI assistance are finding they can create features faster than
their organizations can validate whether anyone wants them. The code arrives.
The decision about whether it was the right code takes two more weeks because
three people need to sign off and one is on vacation.

The constraint isn't the tools. It's the culture, structure, and leadership
needed to deal with complexity and change effectively. Organizations that
spent years adding process to compensate for unclear ownership and broken
communication don't suddenly get those things back when the developer's IDE
gets smarter.

This is the version of the bottleneck that's hardest to fix — and it won't
be solved by buying more licenses.

## What actually changes

I don't think AI makes Agile obsolete. I think it raises the stakes on doing
it well.

If your refinement is weak, the agents downstream will build the wrong
things confidently and quickly. If your Product Owner lacks real authority,
the decision latency that slows you down today will slow you down three times
as much when execution is no longer the excuse. If your team doesn't have
genuine psychological safety, the retrospectives that could identify the
real bottleneck won't surface it.

The teams that will actually get leverage from AI are the ones that already
had the Agile fundamentals in place — not as ceremony, but as a genuine
operating model for navigating uncertainty. For them, AI is fuel. For
everyone else, it's a faster way to build the wrong thing.

The bottleneck moved. The work is the same.

— Roger
